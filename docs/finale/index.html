The Next 90 Days

long enough to finish something, short enough to be concrete

Learning Objectives

  • Write a 90-day plan with three concrete priorities.
  • Know what to read next.

Planning at the Right Timescale (5 min)

Ninety days is the right planning horizon for a project at Jess's scale. It's long enough to finish something substantial and short enough to be concrete. A one-year plan for a two-person research software project is mostly fiction. A two-week plan doesn't force you to think about dependencies and sequencing. Ninety days is where a realistic plan and an ambitious plan can overlap.

Two kinds of work must both appear in the plan. The first is maintenance: keeping the lights on. Bugs, pull request reviews, user questions, dependency updates, compliance reviews. This work is invisible until it isn't done, and it will reliably consume all available time if it isn't explicitly allocated. The second is development: making things better. If maintenance isn't in the plan, it eats all the development time. If development isn't in the plan, the project stagnates.

Getting support you don't have yet requires a one-page impact summary: who uses the project, what it makes possible that wasn't possible before, and what it would take to keep it going. Funders and administrators make resource decisions based on impact, not on elegance of design.

Jess's Situation

Jess's first 90-day plan had five priorities. None of them could realistically be finished in 90 days. She wrote a second plan with three priorities, each with a clear definition of done. The third one still felt ambitious. Her supervisor read it and said: "What's the first thing you'll do on Monday morning for each of these?" For the third priority, Jess couldn't answer. She replaced it with something smaller. The two remaining priorities were both done at the 90-day mark. The third had turned into a year-long project that she was glad she hadn't committed to delivering in a quarter.

"What's the first thing you'll do on Monday morning?" is the single most useful question you can ask about any priority. If you can't answer it, the priority is too vague. Not "work on the documentation" but "write the installation guide section for users on Windows, starting with the GDAL dependency." The more specific, the more likely it gets done.

Exercise A: Draft Your 90-Day Plan (12 min)

Suggested LLM prompt:

"Based on this project health audit [paste rubric from Session 1] and these open issues [paste milestone from Session 8], suggest a 90-day plan with three concrete priorities. For each priority: what will be done, how will I know it's done, and what could prevent it."

Steps:

  1. (3 min) Run the prompt. Note any priority the LLM suggested that you know won't happen because of a constraint it doesn't know: a funding cycle, team capacity, an external deadline.

  2. (9 min) Fill in the provided template:

    • Section 1: Your project's current state (one sentence per health dimension).
    • Section 2: Your three priorities (each: what you'll do, how you'll know it's done, what could stop you).
    • Section 3: Two people you need to talk to (name or role, what you need).

Deliverable: a completed 90-day plan.

Exercise B: Monday Morning Test (8 min)

In pairs:

  1. Read one priority from your plan to your partner.

  2. They ask: "What's the first thing you'll do on Monday morning?" You answer in one sentence. If you can't, the priority is too vague.

  3. Write: "The LLM got [X] wrong because it didn't know [Y]."

Deliverable: one priority that passes the Monday morning test.

Wrap-up (5 min)

Each learner reads one priority from their plan (20 seconds each).

Two questions, answered in writing:

  1. What is the single most useful thing you learned or produced today?
  2. What was included that you don't think you'll use?

Further reading: Fogel Producing Open Source Software, Fournier The Manager's Path [Fournier2017], Manns and Rising Fearless Change [Manns2015], Gawande The Checklist Manifesto [Gawande2009].
