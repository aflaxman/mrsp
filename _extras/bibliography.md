# Additional Bibliography

These bibliography entries are not currently cited in any lesson.
They are retained here as further reading on topics covered in this workshop.

**Achen2017**: Christopher Achen and Larry Bartels,
*Democracy for Realists: Why Elections Do Not Produce Responsive Government*,
Princeton University Press, 2017.
On how political decisions actually get made versus how we imagine they do.

**Brookfield2016**: Stephen D. Brookfield and Stephen Preskill,
*The Discussion Book: 50 Great Ways to Get People Talking*,
Jossey-Bass, 2016.
Practical techniques for facilitating group discussions and meetings.

**Cottom2017**: Tressie McMillan Cottom,
*Lower Ed: The Troubling Rise of For-Profit Colleges in the New Economy*,
The New Press, 2017.
On how institutional structures shape who benefits from education and credentials.

**Ford2016**: Denae Ford, Justin Smith, Philip J. Guo, and Chris Parnin,
"Paradise Unplugged: Identifying Barriers for Female Participation on Stack Overflow",
*Proc. FSE'16*, 2016.
On structural barriers that make online technical communities unwelcoming to newcomers
from underrepresented groups.

**Hoffer2010**: Eric Hoffer,
*The True Believer: Thoughts on the Nature of Mass Movements*,
Harper Perennial, 2010.
On why people join movements, with implications for how open-source communities attract
and keep contributors.

**Huppenkothen2018**: Daniela Huppenkothen et al.,
"Hack Weeks as a Model for Data Science Education and Collaboration",
*Proc. National Academy of Sciences*, 115(36), 2018.
On intensive collaborative events as a model for building community and sharing skills.

**Kahneman2011**: Daniel Kahneman,
*Thinking, Fast and Slow*,
Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011.
On the two systems of human cognition and the systematic biases that result.

**Karabel2006**: Jerome Karabel,
*The Chosen: The Hidden History of Admission and Exclusion at Harvard, Yale, and Princeton*,
Mariner Books, 2006.
On how elite institutions use ostensibly neutral criteria to reproduce social hierarchies.

**Kumar2018**: Srijan Kumar, William L. Hamilton, Jure Leskovec, and Dan Jurafsky,
"Community Interaction and Conflict on the Web",
*arxiv.org*, 2018.
On how online communities interact and how conflict propagates between them.

**Manns2015**: Mary Lynn Manns and Linda Rising,
*Fearless Change: Patterns for Introducing New Ideas*,
Addison-Wesley, 2015.
Practical patterns for persuading organizations to adopt new practices.

**Noble2009**: William Stafford Noble,
"A Quick Guide to Organizing Computational Biology Projects",
*PLoS Computational Biology*, 5(7), 2009.
On practical file and directory organization for research software projects.

**PerezDeRosso2013**: Santiago Perez De Rosso and Daniel Jackson,
"What's wrong with Git?",
*Proc. Onward'13*, 2013.
A principled analysis of design problems in Git's conceptual model.

**PerezDeRosso2016**: Santiago Perez De Rosso and Daniel Jackson,
"Purposes, concepts, misfits, and a redesign of Git",
*Proc. OOPSLA'16*, 2016.
A follow-up proposing an alternative design to address Git's usability problems.

**Prechelt2000**: Lutz Prechelt,
"An Empirical Comparison of Seven Programming Languages",
*IEEE Computer*, 33(10), 2000.
One of the first controlled studies comparing programmer productivity across languages.

**Rivera2015**: Lauren A. Rivera,
*Pedigree: How Elite Students Get Elite Jobs*,
Princeton University Press, 2015.
(Also cited in the Hiring and Firing lesson.) Extended analysis of how elite social
signals shape hiring decisions in professional services firms.

**Robin2017**: Corey Robin,
*The Reactionary Mind*,
Oxford University Press, 2017.
On the intellectual tradition of resisting social change, relevant to understanding
resistance to diversity and equity initiatives.

**Robinson2021**: Nathan J. Robinson,
"Surely We Can Do Better Than Elon Musk",
*Current Affairs*, 2021.
A critique of the "great man" theory of innovation, relevant to discussions of hero
projects and credit in collaborative work.

**Sandel2020**: Michael J. Sandel,
*The Tyranny of Merit: What's Become of the Common Good?*,
Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2020.
A critique of meritocracy as a moral framework for distributing rewards.

**SteinLubrano2024**: Alexis Stein Lubrano,
*Don't Talk About Politics: How to Change 21st Century Minds*,
Bloomsbury Continuum, 2025.
On evidence-based approaches to persuasion and changing organizational norms.

**Wilkinson2011**: Richard Wilkinson and Kate Pickett,
*The Spirit Level: Why Greater Equality Makes Societies Stronger*,
Bloomsbury Press, 2011.
On the social consequences of inequality, with implications for team dynamics and
project health.

**Young1958**: Michael Young,
*The Rise of the Meritocracy*,
Thames and Hudson, 1958.
The satirical novel that coined the term "meritocracy" as a warning, not an aspiration.
